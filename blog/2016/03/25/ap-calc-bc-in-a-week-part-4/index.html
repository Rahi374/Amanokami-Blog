9C Euler's Method

Euler's Method is used to find particular/specific solutions of first-order differential equations numerically.

First we choose a point and find the slope at the point from the given differential equation. Then we use that point and the slope to get the next point (which is one \(\Delta x\) away), and repeat until the question tells us to stop. This allows us to approximate a particular/specific solution.

Basically it draws a sequence of straight lines. Also it might be useful to remember $$\Delta y = \frac{dy}{dx} \times \Delta x$$, though this is kind of intuitional.

I'm not going to do an example since this is pretty simple.

The book does warn us, however, to pay attention to the domain. Otherwise you could foolishly cross a vertical asymptote and end up with a curve not even close to where it should be. (For instance, solutions with negative exponents)

9E3 Logistic Growth

So this comes from section 9E, which are different cases of Exponential Growth and Decay. Case 1 is Exponential growth, case 2 is Restricted growth, and case 3 is Logistic growth.

  • Exponential growth is when the growth rate is proportional to the current population: \(y'=ky\)
  • Restricted growth is when the growth rate is proportional to the difference between a constant (\(A\)) and the current population: \(y'=k(A-y)\)

    Logistic growth is basically a combination of those two, where the growth rate is proportional to both the current population and to the difference between a constant and the current population: $$y'=ky(A-y)$$ This is the Logistic differential equation.

    The solution of this logistic differential equation is: $$y=\frac{A}{1+Ce^{-Akt}}$$

    Apparently in most cases, \(C>1\) so the initial \(A\) is less than \(\frac{A}{2}\)

    In all cases, the exponent of \(e\) is negative, which means as \(t\to\infty\) :

    • \(Ce^{-Akt} \to 0\)
    • denominator of \(y \to 1\)
    • \(y \to A\)

    tl;dr \(A\) is the upper limit of \(y\).

(Pseudo-Graphical) Comparison of growth models

Exponential (unrestricted) growth has \(y\) grow indefinitely, while restricted growth has \(y\) grow approaching \(A\).

Logistic growth is a combination of these two, where it starts off unrestricted (concave up, \(y'' > 0\)), and then reaches a maximum growth rate (\(y'' = 0\)), and then it becomes restricted and approaches \(A\) (concave down, \(y'' < 0\)). This results in an S-shaped curve.

Applications

I'm going to skip applications of logistic growth models since it's mostly intuitional. You would use this model to model exponential growth but a growth that has a limiting factor causing the growth to not be able to grow indefinitely.

Questions

I think these types of questions can be pretty easily solved by plugging in to equations and rearranging them. There's usually a \(y\) where \(t=0\) from which you can find \(C\), and a \(y\) where \(t=1\) from which you can find \(k\) to get the equation, and the question gives you \(A\).

Personally, I think don't bother with the derivation of \(y\) from the logistic differential equation, since it's not that hard to memorize anyway.

Take-home message

  • Logistic growth is unrestricted \(\to\) restricted growth
  • The logistic differential equation is proportional to the current population and the difference between the ceiling and the current population
    • Which is \(y'=ky(A-y)\)
    • And its solution is \(y=\frac{A}{1+Ce^{-Akt}}\)
